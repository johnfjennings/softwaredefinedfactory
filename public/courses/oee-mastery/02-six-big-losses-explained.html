The Six Big Losses

Understanding where your OEE points go

The Framework

The Six Big Losses were defined by Seiichi Nakajima in the Total Productive Maintenance (TPM) methodology.

Every minute of lost production falls into one of six categories, grouped under the three OEE factors.

Loss 1: Equipment Breakdowns

Availability Loss

  • Unplanned stops due to equipment failure
  • Includes mechanical, electrical, and tooling failures
  • Often the most visible and costly loss
  • Typical impact: 5-20% of planned time

Key question: Is the same failure recurring? Root cause analysis is critical.

Loss 2: Setup & Adjustments

Availability Loss

  • Changeovers between products or batches
  • Material loading, warm-up time, parameter adjustments
  • Grows with product variety and short-run production
  • Typical impact: 5-15% of planned time

Key technique: SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die) can reduce changeovers by 50-90%.

Loss 3: Minor Stops

Performance Loss

  • Brief interruptions (typically under 5 minutes)
  • Jams, misfeeds, sensor trips, obstruction clears
  • Often too short to record but add up significantly
  • Typical impact: 5-10% of run time

The hidden killer: Ten 2-minute stops per shift = 20 minutes of lost production that nobody tracks.

Loss 4: Reduced Speed

Performance Loss

  • Equipment running below its designed cycle time
  • Causes: wear, poor materials, operator caution, unknown ideal speed
  • Often the largest and most overlooked loss
  • Typical impact: 5-15% of run time

Warning: Many plants don't know their true ideal cycle time, making this loss invisible.

Loss 5: Process Defects

Quality Loss

  • Parts that don't meet spec during steady-state production
  • Includes scrap (discarded) and rework (re-processed)
  • Cost goes beyond wasted material — includes wasted machine time
  • Typical impact: 1-5% of total output

Remember: OEE counts the time lost making defects, not just the material cost.

Loss 6: Startup/Yield Losses

Quality Loss

  • Defective parts produced during startup, warm-up, or after changeover
  • First-run rejects until process stabilizes
  • Particularly significant in process industries (chemicals, food, plastics)
  • Typical impact: 0.5-3% of total output

Reduction strategy: Standardize startup procedures and document optimal initial parameters.

Which Loss is Biggest?

In a typical factory, the ranking is often:

  1. Setup & Adjustments — especially in high-mix environments
  2. Reduced Speed — the invisible giant
  3. Minor Stops — death by a thousand cuts
  4. Equipment Breakdowns — dramatic but often well-managed
  5. Process Defects — usually tracked carefully
  6. Startup Losses — smallest but not zero

The Pareto Principle

80 / 20

Typically, 80% of your total loss comes from just 2-3 loss categories.

The key to OEE improvement is finding your biggest loss and attacking it first.

Don't try to fix everything at once.

Key Takeaways

  • Every minute of lost production fits into one of the Six Big Losses
  • Availability losses are the most visible; Performance losses are the most hidden
  • Use Pareto analysis to focus on the 2-3 biggest losses first
  • Track all six — even if they seem small today
  • The Six Big Losses framework turns "we need to improve" into "here's exactly where to focus"
