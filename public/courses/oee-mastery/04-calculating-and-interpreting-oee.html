Calculating & Interpreting OEE

Now that you understand the theory, let's walk through a complete OEE calculation step by step. We'll use a realistic scenario and then explore how to interpret the results.

Step-by-Step Calculation

Scenario: CNC Machining Cell — Morning Shift

  • Shift length: 8 hours (480 minutes)
  • Scheduled breaks: 30 minutes (excluded from planned time)
  • Planned production time: 450 minutes
  • Changeover time: 25 minutes (2 changeovers)
  • Unplanned downtime: 40 minutes (tool breakage + material delay)
  • Ideal cycle time: 45 seconds per part
  • Total parts produced: 410
  • Rejected parts: 12 (8 scrap + 4 rework)

Step 1: Calculate Run Time

Run Time = Planned Production Time - Stop Time
Run Time = 450 - 25 - 40 = 385 minutes

Step 2: Calculate Availability

Availability = Run Time / Planned Production Time
Availability = 385 / 450 = 85.6%

You lost 65 minutes out of 450 planned — 25 to changeovers and 40 to unplanned stops.

Step 3: Calculate Performance

Performance = (Ideal Cycle Time × Total Parts) / Run Time
Performance = (0.75 min × 410) / 385 = 307.5 / 385 = 79.9%

At ideal speed, 385 minutes should have produced 385 / 0.75 = 513 parts. You produced 410 — a gap of 103 parts worth of speed and minor-stop losses.

Note: Performance is capped at 100%. If you produce more than the theoretical maximum, it means your ideal cycle time is set too slow and needs updating.

Step 4: Calculate Quality

Quality = Good Parts / Total Parts
Quality = (410 - 12) / 410 = 398 / 410 = 97.1%

Step 5: Calculate OEE

OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality
OEE = 0.856 × 0.799 × 0.971 = 66.4%

OEE = 66.4% — Typical performance. Significant improvement opportunity exists.

Interpreting the Results

The OEE score alone tells you how much you're losing. The three sub-metrics tell you where:

Factor Your Score World Class Gap Interpretation
Availability 85.6% 90% 4.4% Moderate gap — changeover and breakdown improvements needed
Performance 79.9% 95% 15.1% Largest gap — investigate speed losses and minor stops
Quality 97.1% 99% 1.9% Close to target — maintain focus on SPC and error-proofing

Action Priority: Performance is the biggest drag on OEE. Improving Performance from 79.9% to 90% alone would lift OEE from 66.4% to 74.8% — an 8.4-point improvement with no changes to Availability or Quality.

The Sensitivity Effect

A small improvement in any one factor has a multiplied effect on OEE. Consider what happens if you improve each factor by just 5 percentage points:

Scenario Avail. Perf. Qual. OEE Gain
Current 85.6% 79.9% 97.1% 66.4% —
+5% Availability 90.6% 79.9% 97.1% 70.3% +3.9
+5% Performance 85.6% 84.9% 97.1% 70.6% +4.2
+5% Quality 85.6% 79.9% 100% 68.4% +2.0
All three +5% 90.6% 84.9% 100% 76.9% +10.5

Improving the factor with the biggest gap (Performance in this case) gives you the most OEE points per unit of effort.

Common Calculation Mistakes

  1. Including breaks in planned time: Scheduled breaks, lunches, and meetings should be excluded from planned production time. They're not losses — they were never meant to be production time.
  2. Mixing up units: Ideal cycle time in seconds but run time in minutes. Always convert to the same unit before calculating.
  3. Averaging OEE incorrectly: You can't average OEE across lines by simply taking the mean. Weight by planned production time instead.
  4. Ignoring the denominator: If planned time is too short (e.g., a 2-hour run), a single changeover can make OEE look terrible. OEE is most meaningful over a full shift or day.

Tracking OEE Over Time

A single OEE number is a snapshot. The real power comes from tracking trends:

  • Daily OEE trend — Spot sudden drops that indicate new problems
  • Weekly average — Smooth out day-to-day variability to see the real trajectory
  • By product — Which products run best on this equipment? Which cause the most changeover loss?
  • By shift — Do night shifts underperform? Is training needed?
  • Before/after — Quantify the impact of improvement projects
