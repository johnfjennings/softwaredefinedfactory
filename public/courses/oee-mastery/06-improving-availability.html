Improving Availability

Availability measures the proportion of planned production time that the equipment is actually running. It is reduced by two of the Six Big Losses: breakdowns and setup/adjustment time. This lesson covers proven techniques for improving both.

Part 1: Reducing Breakdowns

Breakdowns are the most dramatic form of production loss. A machine stops, production halts, people scramble to fix it. But most breakdowns are preventable.

Total Productive Maintenance (TPM)

TPM is a systematic approach to equipment maintenance that involves everyone — from operators to maintenance engineers. The goal is zero breakdowns.

TPM has eight pillars, but three are most relevant to OEE:

  1. Autonomous Maintenance — Operators perform routine care (cleaning, inspecting, lubricating) rather than waiting for maintenance. When operators own their machines, they catch problems early.
  2. Planned Maintenance — Replace reactive "fix it when it breaks" with scheduled maintenance based on equipment condition or time intervals.
  3. Focused Improvement — Cross-functional teams tackle specific chronic losses using structured problem-solving.

Quick Win: Start with autonomous maintenance. Create a simple daily checklist for operators: 5 things to check, 5 things to clean, 5 things to tighten. This alone can reduce breakdowns by 30-50%.

Root Cause Analysis with 5-Why

When a breakdown occurs, don't just fix the symptom. Ask "why?" five times to find the root cause:

Level Question & Answer
Problem The conveyor belt stopped.
Why 1 The drive motor overheated and tripped.
Why 2 The motor bearing was failing, causing friction.
Why 3 The bearing had no lubrication.
Why 4 The lubrication schedule was missed.
Why 5 There is no visible PM checklist at the machine.
Root Cause Missing visual management for maintenance tasks.

The fix isn't replacing the bearing — it's creating a visual maintenance board with checklists at each machine.

Predictive Maintenance

For manufacturers with IIoT capabilities, predictive maintenance uses sensor data to detect failures before they happen:

  • Vibration monitoring — Detects bearing wear, misalignment, and imbalance weeks before failure
  • Thermal imaging — Identifies hot spots in electrical panels and motors
  • Oil analysis — Reveals contamination and wear particles in gearbox oil
  • Current monitoring — Abnormal motor current draw indicates developing problems

ROI Insight: Predictive maintenance costs more than reactive maintenance per event, but far less in total because it prevents catastrophic failures and unplanned production stops. Use our ROI Calculator to estimate the savings for your operation.

Part 2: Reducing Changeover Time (SMED)

SMED — Single Minute Exchange of Die — is a methodology developed by Shigeo Shingo to dramatically reduce changeover times. Despite the name, the goal isn't necessarily under one minute, but to reduce changeovers as much as possible.

The SMED Process

  1. Document the current changeover — Video-record the entire changeover. Identify every step and its duration.
  2. Separate internal from external tasks
    • Internal tasks — Can only be done while the machine is stopped (e.g., changing a die)
    • External tasks — Can be done while the machine is still running (e.g., getting tools ready, pre-heating, staging materials)
  3. Move external tasks outside the changeover — This alone typically reduces changeover time by 30-50%.
  4. Simplify internal tasks — Replace bolts with quick-release clamps, eliminate adjustments with pre-set fixtures, standardize tooling.
  5. Practice and standardize — Create a changeover procedure, train all operators, and time the process to verify.

Before SMED After SMED (Typical) Improvement

Common Mistake: Manufacturers often reduce changeovers to run larger batches instead of shorter ones. The real benefit of SMED is the flexibility to run more changeovers with less total time — enabling smaller batches, lower inventory, and better customer responsiveness.

Measuring Progress

Track these metrics to verify your availability improvements:

  • MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures) — Should be increasing as breakdowns decrease
  • MTTR (Mean Time To Repair) — Should be decreasing as maintenance teams improve response and repair skills
  • Changeover time — Track the average and spread. Consistency matters as much as speed.
  • Number of breakdowns per week — Simple but powerful trend indicator
  • Availability trend — The ultimate measure on your OEE dashboard
